    What is statistics?

    Simply put, statistics is the science of data. It is a tool used to understand information and then draw conclusions and make predictions based on that data.

    The first seven sections of this web series are going to be based on understanding data – from variables, to distributions, to probability – and the final three sections are going to deal with conclusions that can be drawn from data – a process called statistical inference.

    Material

    This series of webpages starts at the very beginning of any course in statistics and builds up from there. We’ll start by covering all the basics from variables and averages and continue working up in difficulty through inference of tables and regression. The only prior knowledge required to successfully navigate these tutorials is an understanding of high school algebra, although the first few pages will be even simpler than that. By the end of the series, we will have completed the equivalent of a high school Advanced Placement Statistics course or the first semester of statistics in college. While I’ll try to explain the concepts as simply as possible, I’ll also throw in the higher math, including the calculus and formulas behind the calculator, for those who are interested.

    Calculator

    Since I’m modeling this course after an Advanced Placement course in high school or an introductory semester in college, I’ll also include images on how to use a graphing calculator for various statistics operations. Specifically, I’ll reference two such graphing calculators with whom I have experience – the TI-83 and TI-84 series of graphing calculators, and the TI-Nspire series of graphing calculators.

    For maximum compatibility with the tutorials I present on the website, I recommend the TI-84 Plus or the TI-Nspire CX. The TI-83, will work with most of the simple statistical functions, but it is not as well equipped as the TI-84 Plus to handle some of the equations that we will be using at the end of this web series.
